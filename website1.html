Customer retention and revenue growth

This is a look at who actually drives an online retail business.

The store sells a broad mix of low-cost household, novelty, and gift-like products. We used transaction-level purchase data to find which customers matter most, how value builds over time, and where the business should focus its effort.

Scroll to follow the story
Context

A broad, low-cost catalog creates very different customer behaviors.

This retailer sells a wide mix of inexpensive products, and customer behavior is far from uniform. The highest-value customers do not just spend more. They place more orders, build larger baskets, and purchase across a much wider range of products than low-value customers. That behavioral gap is what gives the business its top-heavy revenue structure.

How high-value customers behave differently

Champions are not just bigger spenders. They shop more often, buy more items, and engage with much more of the catalog.

Revenue is concentrated among a small share of customers.

Once the customer base is broken apart, the main pattern appears quickly. A small slice of buyers generates a very large share of revenue. That makes retention and customer quality more important than raw customer count.

Customer value is built through behavior, not just spending.

Not all customers are expected to become high value. In this business, the main separation happens when a customer either returns early or drops out after the first purchase. The segments below are meant to describe those paths through the business, not just rank customers by spend.
Some customers buy once and leave. Others return early, begin to repeat, expand their baskets, and engage more broadly with the catalog over time.
